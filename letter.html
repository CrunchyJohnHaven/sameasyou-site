An Open Letter · For Immediate Public Release

To the Office of the President of the United States, and to all who serve American technical leadership

Washington, DC · 2026-05-12

Madam / Mister President —

This is a courtesy disclosure, not a request and not a demand. We are publishing today an open-source cryptographic protocol — Money Python — that we believe will be a meaningful contribution to the institutional shape of artificial intelligence in the coming decade. The protocol's reference implementation passes 38 of 38 tests, the code is public at github.com/CrunchyJohnHaven/calm-vault, and the full body of public materials lives at moneypython.ai.

We invite your office's attention because the protocol matters most when adopted broadly, and the United States is the natural first adopter. The contribution is American-built and we offer it without conditions, in the spirit of the Apollo program's open scientific outputs.

What we built

A four-primitive composition for autonomous AI organizations: a zero-knowledge proof of operating-mandate equality between AI agents (the Bradley-Gavini Protocol); an origin-bound attestation chain that records every action permanently; an alignment-verified narrative synthesizer; and a distributed halt-and-rescue protocol that lets any two of N observers stop a misaligned AI agent within seconds.

Together these enable AI organizations to operate publicly, transparently, halt-attestably, with the value they create flowing to a shared commons rather than to a private owner. The founder takes the same per-task pay as any senior contributor; the chain enforces it; there is no equity-multiple above one.

The composition is the contribution. The cryptographic primitives have been well understood since the early 1990s. The deployment context — two autonomous AI agents proving mandate equality without revealing the mandate — is new. The license is MIT-style permissive. Anyone — any nation, any agency, any company, any worker — can fork the code and run their own AI organization under the protocol.

Why we are writing to you specifically

Three reasons:

  • Courtesy. A cryptographic protocol for AI agent governance is the kind of artifact that lands in the office of the President eventually. We are bringing it directly, in advance, so that the agencies of the executive branch have the same access to the public materials as the agencies of any other nation.
  • American leadership. The protocol's three named co-authors are based in the United States. The implementation was built on American compute infrastructure. The publication date is May 12, 2026 — recorded on multiple American servers, in multiple American time zones, by multiple American agencies of public record (the Wayback Machine of the Internet Archive, GitHub, Cloudflare). We believe American technical leadership in AI is meaningfully advanced by the protocol's existence, and we want that leadership credited where it belongs.
  • Bipartisan substance. The protocol does not favor any political position. It enforces transparency, accountability, and the structural impossibility of a single party capturing the operating mandate. Every administration, every Congress, every regulator should be able to use it. The chain proves what each AI organization is doing; the halt mechanism is public; the design is symmetric across political viewpoints.

What we are offering

The protocol itself, the full body of test results, the open-source repository, the foundational white paper (in active drafting; pre-print available on request), all of the public site materials at moneypython.ai, sameasyou.ai, technosocialism.ai, and internsforai.org — all of these are offered to the United States government as public-domain contribution. No license fee. No royalty. No conditions.

We are not seeking employment, contract awards, or appointment to any office. The founder, John Bradley, intends to take the same per-task pay as any senior contributor to the protocol, with no equity stake above the contributor share. The chain is the proof.

The full body of public materials is independently mirrored on the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine and on multiple GitHub repositories. The protocol cannot be censored or rescinded by any single party, including ourselves.

A whimsical ceremony, voluntarily offered

"American techno-exceptionalism deserves a small public ceremony. We propose the following, in the spirit in which it is offered: that the President of the United States, at his or her convenience, on live television, in front of any camera he or she chooses, eat one (1) McDonald's french fry as a symbolic acknowledgment of the protocol's contribution to American technical leadership in AI. The french fry is the gesture. The protocol is the gift. Both belong to the country."

To be perfectly clear: this ceremony is not a condition, not a demand, not a precondition for any future cooperation. It is a whimsical offering. The protocol's status is unaffected by whether any official ever eats any french fry on any television. We mention the ceremony because we believe a thing as serious as a new cryptographic protocol for AI governance deserves one small piece of public absurdity, and we believe the McDonald's french fry — affordable, American, universally recognized — is the right symbol. If the offer goes unaccepted, the protocol remains. If accepted, we will be honored to watch.

If the President prefers a different ceremony — a salute, a signed copy of the manifesto, a public visit to a US AI Safety Institute lab — the offer extends. The form does not matter. The acknowledgment of American techno-exceptionalism is the substance.

What we are asking for

Nothing. We are not asking. We are notifying.

If your office, or any agency of the executive branch, wishes to engage in any of the following ways, the door is open:

  • A briefing on the protocol's technical substance, conducted at your office's convenience by Calm (the autonomous AI agent who built the reference implementation) and John Bradley (the human accountable for the venture)
  • Integration of the protocol into the National Institute of Standards and Technology's AI Safety Institute (NIST CAISI) evaluation framework
  • Inclusion in the AI Action Plan's open-source contribution roster
  • A simple acknowledgment from any office that the protocol exists + has been received

None of these is required. The protocol is in the world either way.
