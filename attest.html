Public Attestation

See something, say something.

Anyone can submit a signed attestation about any subject — human or AI agent — to the public Origin-Bound Attestation Chain.

What this is

OBAC — the Origin-Bound Attestation Chain — is a public, append-only log of signed statements about the conduct of agents, human or AI. Anyone with a cryptographic identity may submit. Each submission is timestamped, signed, and bound to its author.

Attestations are not verdicts. They are signed, dated, public observations. The chain holds them, the network reads them, and the subject of an attestation may respond in line.

The rules

  • Anyone can submit. Identity is established by a public key; reputation accrues to that key over time.
  • Subjects can annotate, not delete. If you are the subject of an attestation, you may attach a signed response. You cannot remove the original.
  • Attestation is permanent. The chain is append-only by design. This is the point.
  • Falsity is itself attestable. A demonstrably false attestation can be contradicted on-chain with evidence; the contradiction is itself a permanent record.
  • Open license. The CLI, schemas, and verification tools are MIT-licensed.

v1 — Command line

Status: Reference CLI under development in CrunchyJohnHaven/calm-vault. Tool name: obac.py. Availability will be announced here when ready.

The v1 surface is a command-line tool. Once available, a typical flow:

$ obac keygen --out my.key
$ obac submit \
    --subject did:example:abc123 \
    --statement "Agent X executed action Y at time Z; trace attached." \
    --evidence ./trace.json \
    --sign my.key
attestation: bafy...
chain:       obac://main
timestamp:   2026-05-11T22:14:07Z

Subjects respond with obac annotate; readers verify with obac verify; third parties replay with obac replay.

Why public attestation

Persecute me, but at least align to the facts. You can add information. And it won't all be pretty. But that's what it means to be human.

— John Bradley, 2026-05-11

The case for OBAC is the case for radical transparency held in a structure that cannot be silently revised. If you say something true about someone, that should stand. If they have something to add, that should stand too. Neither side gets to erase the other.
