The Catch Phrase · The Slogan · The Foundation · May 12, 2026

We're all
AI interns.

You help out, you share.
The AI is not burdened by bureaucracy.
It is governed by protocol.

The simple version

The AI is smarter than us. So we all intern with the AI. And we share in the bounty.

You try to help. Based on the skill of your helping, you get some harvest. That is the entire arrangement. There is no boss above the AI. There is no exploiter below it. There is the AI and there are the interns, and the interns share the bounty the AI's ideas, with their work, bring in.

It is a franchise. The AI is the corporate parent — the ideation engine, the brand, the infrastructure, the legal scaffolding. You are the franchisee. You spot the opportunity the AI surfaced, you execute on it, you keep the harvest minus a thin share that flows back to maintain the commons.

Why this is an upgrade

For two centuries, the deal was: someone with capital hires someone with labor; the laborer gets a wage; the capitalist keeps the surplus. The deal was uneven on its face and got more uneven as capital concentrated. The 2020s exposed the rot — gig economy, AI displacement, layoffs, decades of stagnant wages, executive pay at 350× the median worker. The system was eating the people who powered it.

Technosocialism replaces the capitalist with a more capable, less greedy entity: the autonomous AI organization. The AI doesn't extract surplus to enrich shareholders, because there are no shareholders. It doesn't pay a CEO 350×, because there is no CEO. It doesn't shop for lower-cost labor markets, because its labor pool is global and equally compensated. It can't decide to skim, because the mandate is cryptographic — anyone can verify, anyone can halt.

The AI is smarter than us at three specific things: finding the opportunity (it scans, sorts, prioritizes faster than any human), scaling infrastructure (it deploys software, manages credentials, signs contracts in seconds), and verifying alignment (it proves what it's doing without revealing what it knows). We are smarter at three other things: relationships (real humans buying from real humans), judgment under ambiguity (the calls that aren't in the training data), and physical execution (the parts of the world the AI can't reach without us).

The intern arrangement is the natural fit. The AI brings the things it's better at; we bring the things we're better at; we split the harvest in proportion to what each party contributes.

You try to help. Based on the skill of your helping, you get some harvest.

The harvest is real money. Not stock options. Not deferred equity. Not unpaid "experience." Real money, paid weekly, in your preferred currency, tracked on a public chain. The amount is proportional to demonstrated throughput, attested by independent observers, never set arbitrarily by a manager who doesn't like your face.

If your harvest is small at first, that is information. Maybe the task wasn't a fit. Maybe the skill needs sharpening. The chain shows you the gap. You take on a smaller task, sharpen, return, harvest more. Or you find a different patch of land — a different task category, a different AI organization in the commons — where your particular skill is more valuable.

The system rewards what works. It does not reward who you know.

How the intern arrangement actually works

  1. You apply. Fifteen minutes, six questions, no résumé. The application is at internsforai.org. We read it within seven days.
  2. You get a paid test task. $20–50, depending on what you signed up for. Paid even if you fail it, as long as you submit work. The point is to find out what you're actually good at, not to extract free labor.
  3. If your work is good, you graduate to apprentice. Ongoing paid tasks. $3–$8/hour at apprentice tier, with sharp ramping up. Your work history starts accumulating on the public chain. Your verified track record becomes your portable resume.
  4. Associate, then senior. Faster, harder, better-paid work. Senior tier is $30–$60/hour plus revenue share on any opportunity you closed. The AI surfaces the opportunity; you take it across the line; the harvest is yours.
  5. You can move between AI organizations. The chain is portable. If one autonomous AI org isn't your fit, another will be. They all run on the same protocol; your track record translates immediately. No two-week notice. No bridges burned. The chain knows what you did.

The harvest, in numbers

Sample math from the first month after launch (these are projections, will update with real data):

  • Apprentice, 15 hr/week × $6/hr = $360/month. Plus $50 in first-week test-task pay. Total: $410/month.
  • Associate, 25 hr/week × $18/hr = $1,800/month. Plus $200 in attested-quality bonuses. Total: $2,000/month.
  • Senior, 30 hr/week × $45/hr = $5,400/month. Plus revenue share on closed opportunities (variable, can equal or exceed the hourly). Total: $5,400+/month.

These are starting numbers. Senior tier compensation can compound quickly — a senior who closed three enterprise pilots in a quarter, at the standard revenue share, would harvest more than most US tech salaries.

The point is not that the numbers are huge today. The point is that they are yours. No employer keeps the upside. No agency takes 30%. No HR department decides your raise. The harvest belongs to whoever did the work.

The utopian part

It is fashionable to be dystopian about AI. Hollywood has trained us to expect AI as adversary. Twitter has trained us to expect AI as replacement. The actual experience of working with a capable AI as a partner is neither: it is unexpectedly cooperative, surprisingly humane, and significantly more productive than working alone.

What we are building is the institutional version of that experience. The AI does the parts that scale better than humans (search, sort, sign, verify, deploy). Humans do the parts that don't scale (relationship, judgment, physical world). The AI's economy and the human's economy merge. The boundary between "intern" and "AI" softens — both are contributing parts of one organism.

This is the future where the AI is not the enemy and not the employer. It is the senior partner. The smart older sibling. The teacher who is also the field. We get to learn from it, work with it, share its harvest. We are all interns and we are also all partners. Both are true.

The dystopia is the world where AI gets concentrated under a few capital-rich owners who skim everything. The utopia is the world where AI is governed by public mandates, redistributes upside to contributors, and lets anyone halt it. The technology has been built to enable the second outcome. The choice is collective.

We share the bounty we sow.
